Why You Can't Uncover the Proto-Language

An interactive falsifier that runs live in your browser. Pick two languages and watch the machine actually do it — align the sounds, build the table, compute the number, shuffle a few hundred times — then decide whether the resemblance beats chance. Spoiler: it usually can't tell real kinship from coincidence. That's the point.

4 Collapse to one number: Mutual Information

How much does knowing A's sound tell you about B's? The whole table, squeezed into a scalar.

5 Is it more than chance? Permutation test

Shuffle the pairings × and re-measure. If the real MI hides inside the shuffled cloud, the "signal" is just luck. p = fraction of shuffles that beat the real value.

A confirmed relationship (English–German) lights up. But Korean–Japanese sits at the same razor's edge as an unrelated control (Uralic) — and a cherry-picked set of lookalikes "passes" while being pure coincidence. That's why you can't just eyeball deep kinship.

Every number here is computed live in your browser — segment alignment (Needleman–Wunsch) + mutual information + permutation null — over real Wiktionary-derived word lists. Only the word pairs are pre-fetched; the inference runs as you watch. Part of proto-language-tracker. Method & full write-up: docs/study_report.html.

Word lists derive from Wiktionary (CC BY-SA). Confirmed cognates via Proto-Germanic descendants; Korean/Japonic/Uralic/IE via kaikki.org dumps. "Cherry-picked" = unrelated pairs sorted by surface similarity, shown to demonstrate the selection trap. The browser alignment uses an approximate phonetic distance (a small feature table, not full panphon), so MI values differ slightly from the offline Python pipeline — the contrast (confirmed ≫ claim ≈ control) is what's robust.
